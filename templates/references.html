References & sources

SCAR is grounded in existing scientific work, institutional tools, and academic literature. This page documents the main intellectual, methodological, and empirical sources that informed the design of the project.

1. Labo 1.5 – A major source of inspiration ›

The development of SCAR was largely inspired by the work of Labo 1.5, a research collective dedicated to measuring and reducing the environmental footprint of research activities.

The methodological rigor, transparency, and reflexive positioning adopted by Labo 1.5 have been particularly influential for this project.

I had the opportunity to meet and exchange with researchers involved in Labo 1.5, which proved extremely enriching for the conceptual and methodological development of SCAR.

SCAR does not seek to replicate existing tools, but rather to extend their spirit to the scale of individual research projects.

2. Emission factors and carbon data ›

All emission factors used in SCAR are drawn from public, documented, and widely used databases.

  • ADEME – Base Carbone®
    Reference source for mobility, energy consumption, food, and accommodation-related emissions.
  • GES 1point5
    Methodological framework specifically designed for assessing the carbon footprint of research activities.
  • DEFRA (UK Department for Environment, Food & Rural Affairs)
    Complementary factors used for cross-checking and international consistency.

Factors are expressed in kgCO₂e per unit and applied uniformly to ensure internal consistency and comparability.

3. Scenario construction and reduction hypotheses ›

The reduction scenarios implemented in SCAR are based on documented mitigation levers.

  • Modal substitution based on air vs rail comparisons.
  • Digital sobriety informed by ICT and data centre literature.
  • Utility-aware scenarios inspired by proportionality debates.
Scenarios are not forecasts nor prescriptions, but exploratory tools intended to support informed discussion.
4. Scientific literature ›

SCAR is informed by a growing body of scientific literature on academic travel, digital infrastructures, and food-related emissions.

  • Wynes & Donner (2018) – Academic travel emissions.
  • Spinellis & Giannikas (2012) – Cloud computing adoption.
  • Jones (2019) – Data centres and electricity demand.
  • Lannelongue et al. (2021) – Green algorithms.
  • Carlsson-Kanyama & González (2009) – Food and climate.
