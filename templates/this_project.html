This project

SCAR (Scenarios for Carbon Assessment in Research) is both a research-driven and practice-oriented project, designed to make the environmental footprint of academic activities visible, discussable, and interpretable.

1. Genesis of the project ›

SCAR originated during a research internship conducted between January and June 2025. The initial direction and conceptual framing of the project were proposed by Guillaume Dezecache, Research Director at the French National Research Institute for Sustainable Development (IRD), specialising in sustainability sciences. His guidance played a decisive role in shaping both the scientific questions addressed by the project and the methodological orientation of the tool.

Throughout this internship, SCAR progressively took the form of a prototype aimed at quantifying the carbon footprint of research projects while remaining compatible with academic constraints and practices.

At the end of the internship, the decision was made to continue developing SCAR independently, motivated by the conviction that the project is both methodologically relevant and socially necessary.
2. Academic and institutional context ›

SCAR emerges from a growing paradox within academia: while research increasingly focuses on environmental and climate-related issues, the environmental impact of research practices themselves remains poorly quantified and rarely problematised.

Conferences, fieldwork, computational resources, and digital infrastructures are often treated as unavoidable necessities, leaving little room for systematic reflection on their cumulative carbon footprint at the scale of individual research projects.

At the same time, this lack of quantification is becoming increasingly problematic in an evolving institutional context. A growing number of research funding bodies and institutions now explicitly request, or strongly encourage, carbon footprint estimations as part of project evaluation and funding procedures.

In this context, carbon footprint assessment is no longer solely a matter of individual environmental awareness, but increasingly a practical and institutional requirement.

SCAR positions itself as a response to this gap by providing a concrete, transparent, and accessible tool that allows emissions to be measured, visualised, and discussed at the scale of individual research projects.

3. Conceptual and ethical positioning ›

SCAR explicitly rejects a moralising or punitive approach to carbon accounting. Its objective is not to judge researchers, disciplines, or institutions.

Instead, SCAR is grounded in a philosophy of reflexivity.

The ambition of SCAR is not to minimise emissions at all costs, but to make trade-offs explicit where choices exist.
4. Why a scenario-based approach? ›

Rather than producing a single “optimal” footprint, SCAR adopts a scenario-based approach.

Scenarios are intentionally conservative and realistic.

Their role is to highlight margins of action, not to prescribe behaviour.

5. A living and evolving project ›

SCAR is conceived as a living framework.

Beyond its current form, SCAR could be adapted to institutional contexts.

SCAR is not an endpoint, but a starting point for collective reflection on sustainable research practices.
